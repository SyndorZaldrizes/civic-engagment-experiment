Civic Organizations, Cross-Pressured Students, and the Campus as a Laboratory of Democracy

Working dissertation project · Nathan Sanchez · St. Mary’s College of Maryland · 2026

This page presents the full working draft of the dissertation-scale project. It develops the theoretical framework, a campus-based pilot experiment, and paths for scaling into a multi-campus research agenda.

Introduction

Democratic engagement among young adults is widely viewed as both a chronic problem and a crucial site of opportunity. Concerns about low youth turnout, uneven participation, and growing distrust of political institutions have generated a large literature on youth civic and political engagement. At the same time, young people increasingly encounter politics through campuses, workplaces, and digital communities that are themselves structured by organizations and norms. Yet much of the empirical work on student participation remains focused on informational and procedural barriers—how to register students, how to reduce transaction costs—rather than on how organizational cultures shape engagement, especially for those who do not comfortably fit dominant political identities.

This project starts from a different premise: that democratic engagement is not just an individual trait or a function of partisan mobilization, but a relational outcome produced by the civic organizations people inhabit. Following Hertel-Fernandez’s (2024) work on unions and cross-pressured members, I argue that organizations can foster participation among politically ambivalent or ideologically misaligned individuals when they cultivate inclusive norms of participation, frame engagement around shared non-partisan identities, and emphasize local stakes rather than distant partisan conflict. A college campus, with its dense web of student organizations, civic programs, and administrative initiatives, is an ideal setting to test these ideas.

The long-run goal is a multi-campus, multi-method research agenda that could plausibly grow into a dissertation project. As a first step, this paper develops the theoretical framework and proposes a campus-based pilot experiment on democratic engagement among college students, designed to generate evidence for or against the value of scaling the study to a larger number of institutions.

Theoretical Framework: Civic Organizations and Cross-Pressured Members

Classic work on civic associations emphasizes their role in fostering political skills, social networks, and opportunities for collective action. Contemporary research has extended this tradition, showing that youth organizations and structured group experiences promote civic and political engagement by offering meaningful opportunities for participation and socialization into democratic norms. Parallel literatures on framing and political communication emphasize that how issues and participation are framed shapes not only opinions but also political behavior.

Hertel-Fernandez contributes to this conversation by focusing on “cross-pressured” individuals—members whose ideological or partisan preferences do not fully align with their organization’s political activities. In a study of a teachers’ union in a right-to-work state, he shows that local leaders who build strong “cultures of participation” can engage conservative members in union political activities despite ideological disagreement. These leaders frame political action through shared occupational identity (as educators), focus on local issues (such as school funding and working conditions), and cultivate norms that participation is a core expectation of membership. Under these conditions, conservative members report stronger perceptions of representation and higher levels of political engagement.

This perspective challenges more pessimistic views that cross-cutting organizations will demobilize members who disagree with some of their positions. Instead, it suggests that organizational design—how leaders talk about politics, whose identities are centered, what norms are emphasized—can help bridge ideological divides.

Campus-Based Civic Engagement: What We Know and What We Do Not

On college campuses, a parallel literature has focused mainly on voter registration and turnout. Bennion and Nickerson’s (2016) multi-campus field experiment in college classrooms shows that even relatively simple, classroom-based voter registration presentations can increase student registration by roughly six percentage points and turnout by about two to three percentage points. Hill and Lachelier (2014) find that face-to-face mobilization efforts can boost student turnout in campus contexts, reinforcing the idea that personal contact matters. More recently, Michelson (2024) analyzes nationwide efforts to promote student voter registration through the Ask Every Student initiative and related programs, documenting positive associations between exposure to campus registration efforts and reported registration and turnout, especially among first-year students.

This body of work demonstrates that colleges and universities can, under the right conditions, move student participation. However, several limitations remain. First, most studies focus on administrative or procedural interventions: distributing forms in class, sending emails, coordinating tabling, or simplifying access to online registration. Second, the dominant outcomes are registration and turnout, which are important but do not fully capture broader democratic engagement, such as participation in deliberative forums, campus governance, or issue-based organizing. Third, little attention has been paid to the experiences of cross-pressured or politically “out-of-place” students: those whose ideological views or social identities do not match the visible political climate of their campus, and who may be especially wary of overtly partisan appeals.

At the same time, broader work on youth civic engagement emphasizes that adolescents and young adults often encounter democracy through group-based experiences where identities and norms are negotiated, not just through formal political instruction. Research on initiatives like Ask Every Student underscores that students face cognitive and psychological hurdles—uncertainty about where to register, anxiety over choosing a party, skepticism about whether they belong in politics—that extend beyond mere lack of information. When these barriers intersect with campus political cultures that appear homogeneous or hostile, cross-pressured students may be especially likely to withdraw.

Bridging these literatures suggests a new question: can campus organizations and civic programs borrow strategies from union contexts to foster inclusive cultures of participation that bring politically diverse students, including cross-pressured ones, more fully into democratic life?

Research Question and Core Claims

The central research question is:

How do organizational communication frames and participatory norms shape democratic engagement among college students, especially those who experience political cross-pressure or perceive themselves as misaligned with the campus political climate?

The core claims are as follows:

  1. Framing claim: Messages that frame civic engagement in terms of shared campus identity and local stakes will be more effective at promoting democratic engagement than neutral administrative messages or messages emphasizing national partisan conflict.
  2. Norms claim: Messages that explicitly highlight strong descriptive norms of participation (“students here typically participate in…”) will increase engagement by signaling that civic participation is expected and socially supported.
  3. Cross-pressure claim: These effects will be particularly pronounced for cross-pressured students—those who report ideological or partisan views that differ from what they perceive as the dominant campus political orientation, or who feel uncomfortable with visible campus activism.

The Campus Pilot Experiment

To test these claims, I propose a survey experiment embedded in a campus-wide online study of student civic and political engagement.

Population and Sampling

The pilot will be conducted at a single liberal-arts campus. Participants will be recruited via:

  • Announcements in introductory political science and general education courses.
  • Campus-wide email invitations.
  • Outreach through student government and selected, non-partisan student organizations.

The target sample size is approximately 150–250 respondents, sufficient for detecting moderate treatment effects and, more importantly, for generating effect-size estimates for future power analyses.

Measures

The survey will measure:

  • Demographic and background variables: year in school, major, race/ethnicity, gender, first-generation status, residence (on- vs off-campus).
  • Political identity and cross-pressure:
    • Ideology on a standard seven-point scale (from “very liberal” to “very conservative”).
    • Partisan identification, including leaners and non-identifiers.
    • Perceived campus political climate (e.g., “Most politically active students here lean liberal/conservative”).
    • Items capturing discomfort or misfit (“I feel out of place when politics is discussed on this campus”; “I rarely see my views represented in campus political conversations”).
  • Democratic engagement outcomes:
    • Self-reported likelihood of voting in the next national or state election.
    • Willingness to participate in campus civic activities (forums, town halls, petition signing, contacting administrators or elected officials).
    • Internal and external political efficacy tailored to campus politics (“I feel that students like me can influence campus decisions”).
  • Behavioral outcomes:
    • After the experimental treatment, students will be offered a real opportunity to:
      • Sign up for a non-partisan civic engagement mailing list, and/or
      • Indicate interest in attending a specific campus civic event. Click-through and sign-up behavior provide observable measures of engagement that complement self-reports.

Experimental Treatments

Participants will be randomly assigned to one of four conditions, each consisting of a brief, realistic message purportedly from a campus civic engagement office or student organization:

  1. Neutral control: A short message providing generic information about campus resources and upcoming events, with minimal reference to politics or civic engagement.
  2. Partisan conflict frame: A message emphasizing national-level partisan conflict and the importance of “fighting for your side” in a polarized environment, with campus participation framed as a way to influence national politics.
  3. Identity + local stakes frame: A message framing civic engagement as part of being a responsible member of the campus community, highlighting local stakes such as student services, campus funding decisions, and institutional policies. The emphasis is on students’ shared identity as members of the same institution rather than on partisan identity.
  4. Identity + local stakes + norms frame: The same content as condition 3, but with added descriptive norms: for example, stating that many students participate in at least one civic activity each semester, or that students routinely engage in campus decision-making. The aim is to signal that participation is ordinary and expected, not exceptional or deviant.

These conditions operationalize, in a campus context, the distinction between national partisan framing and the organizational strategies identified by Hertel-Fernandez in union settings: identity-based, locally grounded, norm-rich appeals.

Analytical Strategy

The primary analyses will estimate the average treatment effect of each condition on both attitudinal and behavioral outcomes, using linear or logistic regression models as appropriate. To test the cross-pressure claim, I will include interaction terms between treatment indicators and measures of cross-pressure/misalignment (e.g., ideological distance from perceived campus norm, reported discomfort in campus political discussions).

The pilot is not meant to deliver definitive causal estimates at a fine level of precision. Instead, it is designed to:

  • Demonstrate feasibility of recruitment and randomization procedures.
  • Test measurement strategies for cross-pressure and perceived misfit.
  • Generate preliminary estimates of treatment effects, particularly among cross-pressured students, to inform power analyses for a larger, multi-campus study.

Scaling Up: Toward a Multi-Campus Dissertation Project

Building on the pilot, the research can be scaled into a dissertation-level project in at least two ways.

First, a multi-campus field experiment could embed similar framing and norms manipulations into real-world outreach—email campaigns, event invitations, and tabling materials—across a diverse set of institutions (public and private, large and small, ideologically varied). This design would allow for:

  • Random assignment of communication frames at the email or event level.
  • Measurement of actual participation (event attendance, registration for civic programs, joining organizations).
  • Multi-level modeling of treatment effects as a function of campus-level characteristics (overall political climate, organizational density, institutional commitments to civic engagement).

Second, the project could integrate qualitative components— interviews with campus organizers, focus groups with cross-pressured students— analogous to Hertel-Fernandez’s interviews with union leaders, to better understand how organizational actors construct inclusive or exclusive cultures of participation and how students interpret those signals.

In combination, these approaches would move beyond one-off registration drives to a richer theory of how campus civic cultures are built and sustained, and how they affect different segments of the student population.

Conclusion

Existing research demonstrates that youth civic engagement is malleable and that educational and organizational interventions can meaningfully affect registration, turnout, and broader forms of participation. Hertel-Fernandez’s work on unions suggests that cross-pressured individuals are not doomed to disengagement; under the right organizational conditions, they can be drawn into political life through norms of participation, identity-based framing, and locally grounded appeals.

By treating the college campus as a laboratory of democracy, this project seeks to test whether similar organizational strategies can be used to foster democratic engagement among politically diverse students, including those who feel out of place in the dominant campus political culture. The proposed pilot experiment is a first step: a structured, empirically grounded test of how different communication frames and norms shape engagement. Depending on the results, the project can either justify scaling up to a multi-campus, dissertation-level study—if the evidence points to promising effects—or provide clear reasons to reconsider the theoretical assumptions and redesign interventions.

Either outcome advances our understanding of how organizations can cultivate inclusive democratic participation, not by erasing disagreement, but by building civic cultures in which students with different political views still see themselves as entitled—and expected—to show up.

Appendix A. Treatment Messages

Control: Neutral Campus Information

Subject: Campus resources and upcoming opportunities

Dear [Student First Name],

Welcome to another busy semester at [College Name]. We want to make sure you know about a few resources and opportunities that are available to you as a student.

  • Academic support is available through the [Writing Center / Tutoring Center], where you can schedule appointments to work on assignments, study strategies, and time management.
  • Student life offers a wide range of clubs and organizations focused on arts, culture, service, and recreation. You can browse current organizations on the [Student Involvement] webpage.
  • The Counseling and Wellness Center provides confidential support for stress, anxiety, and other challenges you may face during the year.
  • Upcoming events include workshops on study skills, career planning, and financial literacy, as well as social events sponsored by student organizations.

We encourage you to explore these opportunities and make use of the resources that best fit your needs and interests this semester.

Sincerely,
[Office Name]
[College Name]

Partisan Conflict Frame

Subject: Politics is polarized—your voice can help shape the outcome

Dear [Student First Name],

You are living through one of the most polarized moments in modern American politics. National debates over economic policy, reproductive rights, climate change, and democracy itself are sharply divided. In this environment, it is easy to feel that politics is something happening “out there,” decided by others.

But students at [College Name] have the chance to take a stand. When you engage in civic and political activities—attending events, speaking up, voting—you help push our country in the direction you believe is right and push back against those who would move it the other way. Every election, every policy debate, and every public decision is shaped by which side shows up and which side stays home.

By getting involved now, you can make sure your side is represented when it matters. Whether you feel strongly about national issues or simply want your values to be heard, your participation is a way of joining the broader struggle over the future of the country.

Sincerely,
[Office Name]
[College Name]

Identity + Local Stakes Frame

Subject: Your campus, your community, your say

Dear [Student First Name],

[College Name] is not just a place where you take classes. It is a community that you help shape every day. Decisions made here—about housing, tuition and fees, student services, mental health support, campus safety, and how resources are allocated—directly affect your experience as a student.

Many of those decisions are influenced by whether students make their voices heard. When students attend forums, share feedback, meet with campus leaders, and participate in organized efforts, it changes how the institution sets priorities. When students stay silent, decisions are more likely to be made without their perspectives in mind.

Civic engagement on campus is not about taking a particular partisan position. It is about being an active member of the [College Name] community and insisting that students have a meaningful role in shaping the policies and practices that affect their daily lives. Regardless of your political views, you have a stake in how this campus works— and your participation matters.

If you are interested in learning more about opportunities to engage with campus decision-making this semester, you can sign up after this message to receive information about upcoming events and ways to get involved.

Sincerely,
[Office Name]
[College Name]

Identity + Local Stakes + Norms Frame

Subject: Most students want a say in how our campus is run

Dear [Student First Name],

[College Name] is a community, and students play a crucial role in how it operates. Decisions about housing, tuition and fees, student services, mental health resources, campus safety, and other key issues directly shape your experience here.

Students at [College Name] care about these decisions. In recent surveys and events, many students have said that having a voice in campus life is important to them, and each semester students participate in forums, meetings, and initiatives that inform how the college makes choices. Speaking up is not something reserved for a small group of activists—it is a normal part of being a student here.

Civic engagement on campus is not about supporting a particular party or ideology. It is about being an engaged member of the [College Name] community and making sure that student perspectives are part of the conversation before decisions are made. Whatever your political views, your participation signals that students should be taken seriously when it comes to how this place is run.

After this message, you will have the option to sign up for more information about upcoming opportunities to share your views and get involved in campus decision-making. Adding your name is a simple way to join the many students who already take part in shaping our community.

Sincerely,
[Office Name]
[College Name]
