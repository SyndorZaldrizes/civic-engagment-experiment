Civic Engagement Project

A launchpad for future delegates, curious siblings, teachers, and anyone who wants to understand how power really works—and how to use it for something better than just the show.

Welcome, Future Delegates

This is the youth corner of the Civic Engagement Experiment & Lab. The goal here is simple: break big political ideas into pieces you can actually use. No partisan spin, no endless horse-race coverage—just how government works, how decisions get made, and what it looks like when real people push back against “the spectacle” and do democracy on purpose.

You can use this page in a few ways:

  • Students & younger siblings: Start with the explainers and “missions.”
  • Parents & educators: Treat this as a menu of low-stress civic activities.
  • Clubs & delegations: Use the missions as warm-ups for deeper work or simulations.

Quick Civics: What Are We Even Doing Here?

What does government do?

Government isn’t just “people arguing on TV.” At its best, it’s how a community decides together what to do about shared problems:

  • Who gets to use which resources (schools, parks, roads, internet)?
  • What happens when people disagree or break the rules?
  • How money is collected (taxes) and how it’s spent.

If you’ve ever argued over who takes the trash out or who gets the last slice of pizza, you’ve already had a very tiny version of a political debate.

Levels of government (in plain language)

  • Local: Town, city, or county. Handles things like bus routes, zoning, local parks, libraries, and some policing.
  • State: In the U.S., each state has its own legislature, courts, and governor. They deal with education systems, state roads, many criminal laws, and how elections are run.
  • National (federal): Congress, the President, and federal courts handle nationwide issues like immigration, national defense, and federal taxes.

In real life, all three levels overlap. That’s why it gets confusing—and why learning who actually makes which decision is one of your first real “power skills.”

Rights, responsibilities, and power

Democracies don’t run on vibes alone. They run on:

  • Rights: Things the government is not supposed to take from you.
  • Responsibilities: Things you’re expected to do (like follow laws, serve on a jury).
  • Power: Who actually gets listened to when decisions are made. Voting is one piece of that, but so are organizing, speaking, creating, and showing up together.

Your First Civic Missions

These “missions” are designed to be doable in 15–30 minutes each. They work for middle school, high school, or intro-level college students with light tweaks.

Mission 1: Map Your Power

  1. Pick one issue you actually care about (school lunch, buses, rent, climate, etc.).
  2. Ask: Who decides this where I live? Local, state, or national?
  3. Look up:
    • Your city/town council or county commissioners.
    • Your state legislators.
    • Your members of Congress.
  4. Write down one way each level could influence your issue.

For teachers: Have students build a quick “power map” on the board or in a shared doc. It’s chaotic but memorable.

Mission 2: Spot the Spectacle

  1. Pick a news clip, social post, or meme about politics.
  2. Ask:
    • Is this telling me anything concrete about a decision or policy?
    • Or is it mostly about dunking, drama, and making one side look cool?
  3. Label it: information, spectacle, or a mix.
  4. Write one question you’d ask to turn it into something more useful.

The point isn’t to never enjoy the show—it’s to notice when the show is replacing actual decision-making, which is where democratic erosion sneaks in.

Mission 3: Practice a Micro-Debate

  1. Choose a school or local issue with two or more real sides.
  2. In pairs or small groups, assign roles (for/against, or different solutions).
  3. Set rules: each person gets 1 minute to speak, no interruptions, and each side must summarize the other side’s point before responding.
  4. Afterwards, debrief: what felt respectful, what didn’t, and what you learned.

This isn’t about “winning.” It’s training for listening, which is a surprisingly rare civic skill.

For Grown-Ups, Teachers, and Club Advisors

This page is designed as a nonpartisan resource. It doesn’t endorse candidates or specific policies. Instead, it treats civic skills as something you can practice the way you’d practice an instrument, a language, or a sport.

Ways you can use this hub:

  • Classroom warm-ups: Use one “mission” as a 15–20 minute starter before a longer lesson.
  • Club programming: Student governments, MSL delegations, or service clubs can run these as low-stakes activities before bigger simulations or projects.
  • Family conversations: Pick one question (“who makes this decision?” or “is this information or spectacle?”) at dinner or in the car.

For deeper dives into the research behind this hub, see the Journal Memo and Dissertation Project pages.

How This Fits into the Civic Engagement Experiment

This youth hub isn’t just a resource; it’s also part of a larger experiment. The Civic Engagement Experiment & Lab is trying to answer questions like:

  • Which activities actually help young people feel more confident about politics?
  • How do different messages land with students who feel “out of place” politically?
  • Can small, local experiences push back against big, abstract democratic erosion?

If you’re interested in helping test these ideas on a campus or in a classroom, you can explore:

  • Study Details & methods
  • Questionnaires & survey templates
  • Civic Lab services & collaborations

Any real-world use should follow ethical guidelines: informed consent for surveys, respect for students’ privacy, and transparency about what’s being measured and why.
